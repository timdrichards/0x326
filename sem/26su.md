### Summer 2026 · Manning College of Information and Computer Sciences · UMass Amherst

This course is offered entirely online and asynchronous - but, don't worry, you will feel more engaged, welcomed, and connected than any other course you have ever taken. I promise.

## Build the web. For real this time.

Not a survey of buzzwords. Not a slideshow about the internet. An actual, hands-on, ship-real-code course - taught by the person who invented it here, back when jQuery was young and the future was uncertain.

---

## What this course is

The web is the most ubiquitous software platform ever created. Every device has a browser. Most apps are secretly websites wearing a trench coat.

CS326 covers the full stack - from the moment a user types a URL to the database query that answers it. You'll learn how browsers work, how servers think, how data moves, and how to build something that actually runs. Not a toy. A real, dynamic web application built from the ground up over the course of the semester.

This course has been evolving since 2011 and **it has not slowed down**. The web doesn't wait, and neither does the curriculum. What you learn here reflects how the web is actually being built - right now, in 2026, not in some museum exhibit of legacy frameworks.

> **On the old description:** Yes, it mentions AJAX and jQuery. Yes, those words have a vintage quality to them now, like cargo shorts or RSS readers. Ignore that. The underlying ideas are still alive and well - they've just gotten dramatically better haircuts. This course has too.

## The stack you'll actually use

| Technology | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Node.js** | Server-side JS, runtime of choice |
| **Prisma** | Database ORM - SQL without the tears |
| **HTMX** | Hypermedia that doesn't need a framework |
| **Alpine.js** | Client-side reactivity, small and sharp |
| **Tailwind CSS** | Utility-first styling that scales |
| **Jest** | Testing - yes, that's part of the deal |

## What you'll actually do

**Build a full-stack app.** A real dynamic web application, built in sprints, with a team, from scratch.

**Write real architecture.** Route → controller → service → repository. Structure that doesn't collapse under pressure.

**Ship incrementally.** Sprints with real deliverables. No "we'll clean it up at the end." That end never comes.

**Understand the whole web.** HTTP, HTML, CSS, JS, databases, security, and how it all connects in production.

## Git and GitHub - yes, you. all of you.

CS major? Great. Informatics? Welcome. Biology, Physics, English, Comparative Literature, Undeclared? **You belong here too, and this part is especially for you.**

Git is how the entire world manages code, documents, research, data, and collaborative work. GitHub is where that work lives. Together, they are the closest thing the modern knowledge economy has to a superpower - and right now, most people walking around with degrees have never touched either one.

That changes in this course.

By the end of CS326, you will:

- **Version everything.** No more `final_FINAL_v3_actualFinal.docx`. Your work has a history, and you control it.
- **Collaborate without chaos.** Branches, pull requests, code review - the professional workflow used by every serious software team on the planet.
- **Ship with confidence.** Push your code. Watch it deploy. Show someone. Mean it.
- **Have a GitHub profile** that demonstrates real, working projects - not just a transcript line that says you took a class.

This is the part where CS students nod along and everyone else realizes they've been missing something enormous. A biologist who can version-control their analysis scripts, an English major who can manage a collaborative writing project with branches and diffs, a physicist who can share reproducible research - these people are remarkable. They are also rare. You could be one of them.

> Git will frustrate you exactly once, deeply, and then you will never go back. That is a promise and a warning in equal measure.

## ⚠ A word of warning

This course says "web programming" in the name. That's not decorative. Programming is the central activity. You will write code, debug code, read other people's code, and occasionally despair at code.

**Prior JavaScript experience is strongly recommended** - not because we'll quiz you on trivia, but because you'll be writing JS from day one and the learning curve has a sense of humor.

This course will make you uncomfortable in productive ways.

You'll leave understanding the web not as a user, but as someone who builds it. You'll leave with a GitHub profile showing real work, real commits, and real projects - the kind of thing you can open on your phone and show your parents, your roommate, a recruiter, or a skeptical professor in an entirely different field, and watch their expression change.

Come ready to work. Come ready to ship. Come ready to debug something at 11pm that was definitely working an hour ago. And come ready to `git push` something you're genuinely proud of.
